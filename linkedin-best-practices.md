# LinkedIn Posting Best Practices — Baseline Layer

*Generic floor for AI-drafted posts. A person's voice profile, funnel-stage goal, and learned posting patterns override any rule below where they conflict.*

**Note on generic social-media templates:** default "social post" checklists (hook in first line, 3-5 hashtags, emoji appropriate to brand, generic engagement-prompt CTA) are the starting assumption most drafting tools reach for. This document deliberately overrides three of those defaults with current, sourced evidence: hashtags no longer reliably drive reach on LinkedIn (below), emoji should be used sparingly rather than "as fits the brand," and CTAs should differ by funnel stage rather than defaulting to a single generic engagement prompt. Where this doc is silent, generic platform-appropriate formatting (professional-but-conversational framing, paragraph breaks for readability) still applies.

## Hook / Opening Line (first 1–3 lines)

- The first ~2 lines are the entire game: LinkedIn truncates posts behind "…see more" at roughly 140–210 characters depending on device (mobile cuts sooner). Write the hook to land *before* that cut, not just at the top of the post.
- Lead with a claim, tension, number, or question — not a scene-setter ("Last week I…") or a throat-clear ("I'm excited to share…"). The hook must earn the click on its own; it is evaluated before anything else in the post.
- Keep it short and plain: one sentence or two clipped lines, sounding like something a person would say out loud, not a headline or press-release voice.
- Whatever the hook promises (a number, a story, a contrarian take), the body must deliver on immediately — a strong hook with a generic payoff reads as bait and hurts trust (and, per the dwell-time mechanic below, hurts distribution too).

## Structure & Formatting

- Short paragraphs: 1–3 sentences per block, one idea per block. Break lines often — LinkedIn's mobile-first, narrow-column reading pattern punishes dense paragraphs.
- Use white space and line breaks as pacing/emphasis tools, not decoration. Avoid walls of text under any circumstance.
- Total length: no single validated "magic number" exists, but converging practitioner data points to roughly 900–1,600 characters (~150–250 words) as a common sweet spot for text posts — long enough to build dwell time, short enough to finish. Treat this range as a reasonable default, not a hard rule; a punchy 3-line post and a 2,000-character story can both outperform it depending on the hook and topic.
- Native formats LinkedIn currently rewards with outsized reach/engagement relative to plain text: multi-slide document/carousel posts and native video. If the content lends itself to either, prefer them over a plain text wall — this is one of the more robust, large-sample findings available (see Sources).
- End with one clear line (a takeaway, a question, or a CTA) rather than trailing off — the last line is a re-engagement trigger for comments.

## Hashtags & Emoji — evidence is mixed, treat with caution

- **Hashtags:** LinkedIn shut down dedicated hashtag-follow pages in October 2024, which removed most of their historical discovery/reach function. Current guidance from platform-adjacent sources (Sprout Social, etc.) is 3–5 relevant hashtags used as topical routing signals, not 10+ stuffed keywords — but there is no strong current evidence hashtags meaningfully move reach anymore. Default: 0–3 specific, genuinely relevant hashtags; never treat hashtag count as a lever worth optimizing hard.
- **Emoji:** Directional evidence (not rigorously peer-reviewed) suggests light emoji use (1, maybe 2) as visual bullets/emphasis correlates with a small engagement lift, with returns flattening or reversing beyond that. Independent research on emoticons in professional online communication also shows senders are perceived as "friendlier and more competent" in moderation. Treat this as *weak-to-moderate* evidence: use emoji sparingly and only where they aid scannability (e.g., as bullet markers), never to decorate every line.

## CTA Conventions

- A CTA should invite a specific, low-friction action that keeps the reader on-platform: a pointed question, "which of these have you tried," "tag someone who needs this," or a "comment [word] and I'll send you X" exchange.
- Avoid generic or transparently manipulative asks ("Like if you agree," "Repost if you're smart") — LinkedIn's own ranking systems and most practitioner guidance treat these as engagement-bait patterns that underperform or get suppressed.
- **TOFU (awareness):** CTA should invite discussion/opinion-sharing that broadens reach and signals relevance to new audiences — open questions, "agree or disagree," invite people to share their own experience. Goal is conversation, not conversion.
- **BOFU (conversion):** CTA can be more direct (book a call, DM me, get the guide) but should still avoid a raw external link in the post body — route through "comment to receive," a native LinkedIn form/newsletter, or a link in the first comment instead.

## Algorithm & Dwell Time — what's confirmed vs. contested

- **Confirmed (LinkedIn's own engineering blog, Oct 2024):** LinkedIn explicitly uses "dwell time" — both on-feed time (how long a post stays in view while scrolling) and post-click time (time spent after expanding/clicking) — as a ranking signal. A "click bounce" (very fast return to feed after clicking) is treated as a negative signal even if the post got likes. This directly rewards posts that are worth pausing on: strong hook, easy-to-scan structure, genuine substance.
- **Contested — external links:** LinkedIn is widely believed to suppress posts with links in the body because they push users off-platform. The *size* of that penalty is genuinely disputed in current data: a large independent 2025 analysis (Just Connecting / Richard van der Blom, ~1.8M posts) found a single link cost about 18.8% of reach, and by 2025 the effect had reportedly reversed to a slight net positive as LinkedIn eased the penalty; separately, Buffer's 2M-post 2025 study found link posts still had the *lowest* median engagement rate of any format. Widely repeated figures of "60–68% reach loss" trace to lower-quality marketing blogs and are not well substantiated — do not treat them as fact.
- **Practical default given the disagreement:** keep links out of the post body when the goal is reach/engagement (TOFU/MOFU); it's lower-risk to route via "link in comments" or a native format, but don't assume a catastrophic penalty — a well-written post with a link is not doomed.

## Posting Cadence

- The best available large-sample evidence (Buffer, 2M+ posts / 94,000+ accounts, 2025) shows posting *more* consistently improves per-post reach and engagement rate on LinkedIn, up to at least 11+ posts/week — unlike most other platforms, LinkedIn does not appear to punish frequency.
- The steepest gain is the jump from 1x/week to 2–5x/week (+~1,182 impressions/post, +0.23pp engagement rate in that study). Gains continue at 6–10x/week and 11+/week but with diminishing marginal returns relative to the effort/quality tradeoff.
- Default recommendation: treat 2–5 posts/week as the floor for meaningful algorithmic "activity" credit; scale up only if quality/voice consistency can be maintained — a low-quality high-frequency stream is not supported by the same data as a quality-held-constant one.
